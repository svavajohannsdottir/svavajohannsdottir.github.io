On Sculpture

For me the sculpture is the ultimate art form. In it I find a necessary physical balance and also—and perhaps therefore inevitably—aesthetic balance. The sculpture depends on surrendering to nature’s laws; complex physical equilibrium gives way to aesthetic appreciation by somewhat ordered irregularities. For some years I have studied these irregularities. I have found some in art and I have found more in nature. As a student of the arts I have wondered what makes beauty in a simple irregular alignment or composition. What makes my eyes play with ease, makes me find not only beauty but also a long-awaited rest. I have been surprised to find these same alignments and compositions everywhere in nature. Nature’s core existence evolves around the search for equilibrium; her desire for balance gives way to ordered irregularities. Perhaps that is why nature is always beautiful and is never considered to be kitsch. I have tried to adopt these irregularities in my artistic representations and I have used them as a weapon when imitating natural phenomenons.

Water is what I have spent most of my professional career studying and imitating. The complex equilibrium composing a river, waterfall or mist rising as water hits a water surface. The fluid dynamics consist of endless possibilities—infinite paths within the finite space of a turbulent flow. An insignificant looking turbulent flow containing infinity. Observing it, my eyes play with ease and I rest.

Virginia Woolf portrayed this infinity almost one hundred years ago. Woolf writes of a view out of a window; a flow of people and cars through the streets of London and in it “no two people are ever alike”. A sight which her imagination had invested with a rhythmical order, eased strain and restored unity to her mind. Of the flow she writes:

At this moment, as so often happens in London, there was a complete lull and suspension of traffic. Nothing came down the street; nobody passed. A single leaf detached itself from the plane tree at the end of the street, and in that pause and suspension fell. Somehow it was like a signal falling, a signal pointing to a force in things which one had overlooked. It seemed to point to a river, which flowed past, invisibly, round the corner, down the street, and took people and eddied them along, as the stream at Oxbridge had taken the undergraduate in his boat and the dead leaves. Now it was bringing from one side of the street to the other diagonally a girl in patent leather boots, and then a young man in a maroon overcoat; it was also bringing a taxi-cab; and it brought all three together at a point directly beneath my window; where the taxi stopped; and the girl and the young man stopped; and they got into the taxi; and then the cab glided off as if it were swept on by the current elsewhere. (Woolf, W. (2004). A Room of Ones Own (33th edition). London: Penguin Books. p. 110-111.)

Reading her words I feel the rhythmical order; and strain has been eased from my body. I have unity restored to my mind as when I have looked at leaves dancing with the wind or appreciated a beautiful painting. It is the search of a complex body for equilibrium, which gives rise to somewhat ordered irregularities, which gives rise to beauty. The nature of the sculpture is to combine physical and aesthetic equilibrium, each complementing the other. With that being said I think the sculpture, if successfully composed, is destined to guide beauty to the heart of the beholder.
